Conclusion

We had set out to prove the importance of Mergers in galaxy evolution. Galaxy mergers are hypothesized to be the source of how galaxies take up mass. Mergers in the past have resulted in the galaxies we see today. To prove the existence of mergers and how mergers have changed the course of galaxy evolution over time, we turned our attention to observational methods and Theoretical simulations.

The CAS Method is used in astronomy to quantify the morphological structure of a galaxy. Each Quantity corresponds to a numerical value for a given galaxy. The CAS parameters can be used to identify mergers and hence calculate merger fraction, which is the number of mergers selected within a given redshift bin and stellar mass limit ( $N_m$) divided by the total number of galaxies within the same selection ( $N_T$). Merger fractions can be used to calculate merger rates which can then be used to calculate the mass accreted onto a galaxy in a given time scale. For z< 3, the average mass accreted was around 0.5 of the original mass. For z between 1-3, a galaxy undergoes about four major mergers. These results agree with the current theoretical prediction by models. Merger rates also help us form merger history plots giving us insight into galaxy evolution in the past.

The merger fraction evolution gave a power law describing the change of merger fraction with redshift. The value of the power was found to be m~3 for z~1. We also found out that the CAS parameters peaked at z~2.5. This is the point where SFR peaked in the timescale of the universe.

These results undoubtedly prove that mergers play an important role in galaxy evolution. Concepts such as galaxy quenching and the formation of AGN, which I had to skip over to keep the review short, are hypothesized to owe their existence to galaxy mergers. I would like to update this review with more recent papers and include the above-stated phenomenons and their relationship with Mergers. Mergers undoubtedly play a huge role in galaxy evolution.
