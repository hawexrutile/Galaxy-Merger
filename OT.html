Observational Techniques

The measurements of the history of galaxy merging is sometimes thought to be ambiguous.

Merger Fractions

The basic merger fraction ($f_m$) is calculated as the number of mergers selected within a given redshift bin and stellar mass limit ( $N_m$) divided by the total number of galaxies within the same selection ( $N_T$).

Nonparametric Measurements

The concentration (C), asymmetry (A), and clumpiness (S) (CAS) approach is now the most used methodology to measure galaxy structure in a nonparametric way. These values are intended to capture the key characteristics of these galaxies' underlying structures. These nonparametric parameters are ideal for determining galaxy evolution over numerous epochs since they are detectable out to high redshifts.

Observational techniques are an important analytical component of galactic merger studies as they help us in quantifying mergers in the universe, giving rise to quantities like merger rates, merger history, and merger fractions. These quantities better help us understand the nature and importance of mergers in galaxy evolution and, inturn help build better simulations. There are two major classes of methods used to observationaly study mergers.

I Pair studies

It is the oldest and most straightforward observational method which involves looking for galaxies in pairs (e.g., Lin et al. 2004). However, pair studies require spectroscopy of complete samples of field galaxies, and have only successfully been applied up to $z \sim 1.4$.

II Morphology study

The other method, particularly useful at $z > 1$, is to look for galaxies that are peculiar in their morphology and to use this as a measure of the merger fraction. (Conselice et al. 2003).

There are, however, some limitations to how these classifications can be used at higher redshifts, as it is not clear how the apparent morphology of a galaxy changes under the influence of redshift effects as opposed to real evolution.

One automatic method for finding mergers is the CAS approach (Conselice et al. 2003), in which merging galaxies follow the simple condition $$ \color{#e44c65}(A > 0.35) \& (A > S) $$ Since galaxies involved in the merger process are only quantitatively asymmetric for about a third of the lifetime as a merger, the fraction of identified mergers is 50% of the toatal in a given limit. The CAS Method results in low contamination.

The Merger History

Determining the history of galaxy interactions, as opposed to active mergers, is more difficult. Since close galaxies will interact without necessarily merging, looking for pairs of galaxies provides us a decent picture of the likely interaction history. According to recent research, the history of star formation is significantly influenced by the history of mergers and interactions.

II Galaxy merger rate evolution

The merger fraction is converted into a galaxy merger rate through the use of the merger timescale $\tau_{m}$, which can be derived through simulations Lotz et al.(2008b) . Using the merger timescale, the merger rate per galaxy is the average amount of time between mergers for a galaxy)within the given selection property and redshift range). Its defined by $\Gamma$ $$\color{#e44c65} \Gamma(M^*, z) = \frac{\tau _m}{f_{gm}}$$. The value of $\Gamma$ is in units of gigayears.

The number of mergers that occur between various redshifts can be calculated by integrating the inverse of $\Gamma$ over redshift $$\color{#e44c65} N_{\text {merg }}=\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \Gamma^{-1} d t=\int_{z_1}^{z_2} \Gamma^{-1} \frac{t_H}{(1+z)} \frac{d z}{E(z)} . $$

III Galaxy Mass Acretion Rate

The amount of stellar mass added from mergers to galaxies over time can be calculated using the merger fraction . The total amount of stellar mass accreted into a galaxy in a given redshidt and mass limit is calculated as $$\color{#e44c65} M_{*, M}=\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \int_{M_1}^{M_2} M_* \times \frac{f_m^{\prime}\left(z, M_*\right)}{\tau_{\mathrm{m}}\left(M_*\right)} d M_* d z, $$ where $ \tau_m (M_∗)$ is the merger timescale, which depends on the stellar mass of the merging pair (Bluck et al. 2012). From this we calculate the total integration of the amount of mass assembled through merging for galaxies with stellar masses $M_∗ > 10^{11}M_\odot$.
