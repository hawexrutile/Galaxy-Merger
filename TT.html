Theoretical Techniques

Simulations of galaxy formation are critical for interpreting and understanding the meaning of structural indices of galaxies.

Introduction

According to one of the earliest computer models of galaxy formation by Toomre & Toomre (1972) the strange morphologies of galaxies shown in, for example, the Arp (1966) atlas were owing to systems experiencing significant mergers rather than any other reason.

One of the first studies employing the TREESPH hydrodynamical approach from Hernquist & Katz (1989) to specifically show how the merging process caused the unusual galaxies found in deep HST imaging was published by Mihos & Hernquist (1996) .

CAS integration

Conselice was the first to use these numerical models of structural evolution to apply quantitative structural approaches like CAS Conselice 2006b. Conselice's simulations are straight forward because they exclude the impacts of star formation and dust, which are known to cause significant morphological changes.

In his numerical simulations, Lotz et al.(2008b) includes the first observations of the CAS and Gini/M20 parameters along with ancient and young stars, star formation, gas, and dust. When imagining the appearance of these galaxy mergers, Lotz uses simulations of galaxies based on the GADGET/N-body/hydrodynamical theories.

CAS integration Improvement

Lotz et al.(2008b,2010)

Lotz continues to look into the locations in the CAS and Gini/M20 parameter spaces for mergers in various circumstances and various characteristics of the merging galaxies. Based on where these simulated galaxies lie in these nonparametric structure spaces, they examine the timetable for how long they appear to have "merged." Additionally, these works explore how the dust, viewing angle, orbital parameters, gas characteristics, supernova feedback, and overall mass affect the structural merger timescale. According to Lotz, the majority of parameters, such as total mass, supernova feedback, viewing angle, and orbital characteristics of mergers, have just a little impact on the estimated timescales.

Lotz found that the asymmetry timescales for gas-rich major mergers are $0.2 \sim 0.4$ Gyr and 0.06 Gyr for minor mergers . The Gini/M_{20} timescales are $\tau _{m} = 0.2 \sim 0.4$ Gyr. Given that these durations are pretty short, it is likely that the observed merger fraction translates into a high merger rate.

According to Lotz's research, the asymmetry index is sensitive to large mergers with ratios of 1:4 or below. However, the Gini/M20 parameters are sensitive to mergers with ratios as low as 1:9, allowing for the probing of more minor mergers.

GADGET/N-body/hydrodynamical simulations

GADGET is a free programme for massively parallel computers with distributed memory that simulates cosmological N-body/SPH phenomena.  GADGET can be applied to a variety of astrophysically relevant issues, such as galaxy mergers and collisions as well as the emergence of large-scale structure in the universe.

Λ-CDM Model

There are many reasons to expect that galaxies form through mergers. Perhaps the most overwhelming is that the largely accepted cosmological model a – Λ dominated Cold Dark Matter (CDM) based universe – explicitly predicts that galaxies should form in the merger process. If mergers are not responsible for the majority of massive galaxy formation, it would require a changing of our assumptions concerning dark matter and the nature of baryonic physics.
