Observational Results

The merger fraction history is the basic observable that reveals how mergers are changing and evolving through cosmic time.

The Merger Fraction Evolution

The inferred merger fraction is seen to grow with redshift in this graphic. Usually, this rise fits as a power law of the following form: $$\color{#e44c65} f_{\mathrm{m}}=f_0 \times(1+z)^m $$ where $f_0$ is the merger fraction at $z = 0$ Here, m is the power-law index used to measure mergers, and $f_0$ is the merger fraction at $z = 0$. In general, the merger history grows at higher redshifts more steeply the greater the value of $m$.

Merger Rate Evolution

The result of calculations by Conselice (2009) and Bluck et al. (2009)shows that the number of major mergers a galaxy undergoes between $z = 3$ and $z = 1$ is $4.3 \pm 0.8$ major mergers for galaxies selected with stellarmasses $M^∗ > 10^{10}M_\odot$.

Mass Acretion Rate

For a $z < 3$ mass complete sample at this limit, Conselice et al. (2013) find a value $M_{∗,M} /M_∗(0) = 0.56 \pm 0.15$, where $M_∗(0)$ is the initial average stellar mass at $z \sim 3$, and $M_{∗,M}$ is the average amount of stellar mass accreted in mergers at z = 1–3.For systems with stellar mass ratios as low as 1:100 for an average massive galaxy following a merger, this ratio is the fractional amount of stellar mass gained due to both major and minor mergers adjusted for constant co-moving density (Conselice et al., 2013). We can determine how much gas is added to galaxies during the merger process by looking at the number of mergers. There is a significant shortfall compared to the star formation rate, which must be made up of gas from the intergalactic medium. The gas accretion rate from the intergalactic medium is estimated to be approximately $100M_{\odot year^{-1}$ using this figure and the observed star-formation rate within these galaxies, contributing nearly the same amount of stellar mass to these galaxies as mergers (Conselice).
